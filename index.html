Part 1: Core Principles of Professional Ethics

These nine principles form the foundation of ethical professional conduct and decision-making.

🎯

1. Accountability

Taking responsibility for one's actions, decisions, and their consequences. Professionals must be answerable to stakeholders, clients, and the public.

  • • Accept responsibility for outcomes
  • • Acknowledge mistakes openly
  • • Document decisions and rationale
  • • Report to appropriate authorities
👁️

2. Awareness

Being informed and conscious of professional standards, regulations, and the broader impact of one's work on society and the environment.

  • • Stay current with industry standards
  • • Understand regulatory requirements
  • • Recognize potential impacts
  • • Monitor changes in the field
⚖️

3. Ethics

Adhering to moral principles and professional codes of conduct. Making decisions based on what is right rather than what is merely legal or profitable.

  • • Follow professional codes
  • • Make principled decisions
  • • Maintain honesty and integrity
  • • Avoid conflicts of interest
🔗

4. Multidisciplinary

Collaborating across different fields and disciplines to achieve comprehensive solutions. Recognizing that complex problems require diverse expertise.

  • • Collaborate with other experts
  • • Value diverse perspectives
  • • Integrate knowledge from multiple fields
  • • Build cross-functional teams
⚖️

5. Proportionality

Ensuring that actions and responses are appropriate and balanced relative to the situation at hand. Avoiding excessive or insufficient measures.

  • • Balance costs and benefits
  • • Scale responses appropriately
  • • Consider resource allocation
  • • Avoid overreaction or negligence
🧩

6. Integration

Incorporating ethical considerations into all aspects of professional work, not treating ethics as a separate concern but as an integral part of every decision.

  • • Embed ethics in processes
  • • Align systems and values
  • • Create cohesive approaches
  • • Connect theory with practice
⏱️

7. Timeliness

Acting promptly and meeting deadlines. Recognizing that delayed actions can lead to missed opportunities or increased harm.

  • • Respond promptly to issues
  • • Meet established deadlines
  • • Prioritize urgent matters
  • • Prevent delays in critical processes
📊

8. Assessment

Regularly evaluating actions, outcomes, and processes to ensure continuous improvement and compliance with ethical standards.

  • • Conduct regular evaluations
  • • Measure against standards
  • • Identify areas for improvement
  • • Use evidence-based methods
🤝

9. Equity

Ensuring fairness and justice in all professional dealings. Treating all stakeholders with equal respect and providing equal opportunities.

  • • Ensure fair treatment for all
  • • Address systemic inequalities
  • • Promote inclusive practices
  • • Distribute resources fairly

Part 2: Ethics and Legal Issues

Understanding the legal framework that governs professional practice is essential for ethical conduct.

📋 Legal Agreements

Confidentiality Agreements (NDA)

A Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA) is a legally binding contract that establishes a confidential relationship between parties.

Purpose: Protect sensitive information from unauthorized disclosure

Elements: Definition of confidential information, obligations, duration, exclusions

Types: Unilateral, Bilateral, Multilateral

Breach consequences: Legal action, damages, injunctions

Trade Secrets

Trade secrets are proprietary information that provides a competitive advantage and is kept confidential.

Examples: Formulas, processes, customer lists, marketing strategies

Protection: No registration required, but must maintain secrecy

Duration: Protected as long as secrecy is maintained

Laws: Defend Trade Secrets Act, UTSA

Discovery Agreements

Legal process where parties exchange relevant information before trial in civil litigation.

Types: Interrogatories, depositions, document requests, admissions

Purpose: Gather evidence, narrow issues, avoid surprises at trial

Obligations: Duty to preserve, produce, and not spoliate evidence

Protective orders: Limit disclosure of sensitive information

Non-Compete Agreements

Contracts restricting employees from competing with employers after employment ends.

Restrictions: Time period, geographic area, scope of activities

Enforceability: Varies by jurisdiction; must be reasonable

Consideration: Must offer something of value (job, raise, training)

Exceptions: Some states ban or heavily restrict them

💡 Intellectual Property and Fair Use

What is Intellectual Property?

Intellectual Property (IP) refers to creations of the mind: inventions, literary and artistic works, symbols, names, images, and designs used in commerce. IP is protected by law through patents, copyrights, trademarks, and trade secrets.

Categories of IP:

  • Industrial Property (patents, trademarks)
  • Copyright (artistic/literary works)
  • Trade Secrets
  • Geographical Indications

Why IP Matters:

  • Encourages innovation
  • Protects creators' rights
  • Enables economic growth
  • Promotes fair competition

Fair Use Doctrine

Fair use is a legal doctrine that permits limited use of copyrighted material without acquiring permission from the rights holders. It is determined by four factors:

1. Purpose and Character

Is the use transformative? Commercial or educational?

2. Nature of the Work

Is the original work factual or creative?

3. Amount Used

How much of the work was used? Was it the "heart" of the work?

4. Market Effect

Does the use harm the market for the original?
